Correlation vs. Causation: A Trap for Young Players

By Feda Mohammadi | September 1, 2025

Introduction: The Most Important Rule in Data

In the world of data analysis, there is one idea so important, yet so often misunderstood, that it creates a clear line between a beginner and an expert. This idea is the difference between correlation and causation. As students who work with both economics and data, understanding this difference is more than just a school exercise. It is a vital thinking skill that helps you avoid making silly or even dangerous mistakes. Getting this wrong can lead to absurd conclusions, wasted money, and bad decisions in policy and business. It is a fundamental trap that every new analyst must learn to recognize and avoid.

Correlation Explained: Just a Connection

At its heart, correlation is a simple concept. It is a statistical measurement that tells us there is a relationship between two things, or variables. When one variable changes, the other variable tends to change in a consistent way. We see a few types of correlation:

  • Positive Correlation: This is when one variable goes up, and the other variable tends to go up too. A simple example is the connection between hot weather and sales of ice cream. As the temperature increases during the summer, ice cream shops usually sell more of their product.
  • Negative Correlation: This is when one variable goes up, and the other variable tends to go down. Think about the relationship between how many hours a student studies for a test and how many mistakes they make. In general, more time spent studying is connected to fewer errors on the exam.

Finding a correlation is often the exciting first step when looking at data. It is easy to calculate and can show us interesting patterns that are worth looking into more deeply. The biggest danger is stopping at that point. It is very tempting to see a strong connection and immediately decide that one thing must have caused the other. This is where the real problems start.

The Bold Claim of Causation

Causation, or causality, is a much stronger and more difficult thing to prove. It means that a change in one variable directly *causes* a change in another. There is a real mechanism or process that links the two. For a relationship to be causal, we need to be sure that the effect would not have happened without the cause, and that there is not some other hidden factor involved. For instance, when you flip a light switch, it causes the light to turn on. When you press the gas pedal in a car, it causes the car to speed up. There is a direct and provable link.

Common Mistakes: The Hidden Variable

Let's look at the most famous example used to explain this problem. If you study data from a city, you will almost definitely find a strong positive correlation between ice cream sales and the crime rate. On days when more ice cream is sold, the crime rate is also higher. A beginner analyst might conclude that eating ice cream makes people commit crimes. The recommendation from this analysis would be ridiculous. Should we forbid ice cream to make our cities safer?

Of course, the answer is no. This is a perfect example of a hidden or "confounding" variable. In this case, the hidden variable is the weather, specifically the heat. When it is hot outside, two separate things happen. First, more people buy ice cream to feel cooler. Second, more people are outside of their homes, walking around and interacting with others, which creates more opportunities for arguments and crime. The hot weather causes an increase in both ice cream sales and crime. This creates a fake correlation between them, but one does not cause the other. The ice cream is not the problem at all. The heat is the common cause that affects both things.

Key Takeaways

  • Correlation is Not Causation: The most important rule in data analysis is that just because two things are related does not mean one causes the other.
  • Look for Hidden Variables: Always ask yourself if a third, unobserved factor could be causing both variables to change at the same time.
  • Causation is Hard to Prove: Claiming one thing causes another requires strong evidence, often from carefully designed experiments, not just observational data.

  • Wheelan, C. (2013). *Naked Statistics: Stripping the Dread from the Data*. W. W. Norton & Company.

Landing Your First Job in Data Science

By Feda Mohammadi | August 28, 2025

Introduction: Starting Your Career

Beginning your career in data science is an exciting step. Companies in almost every industry are looking for people who can work with data to find valuable insights. However, finding your first job can feel challenging because there are so many different roles and titles. You might see jobs called Data Analyst, Business Intelligence Analyst, or Junior Data Scientist. It can be hard to know where to start. This guide will help you understand the different jobs available and give you a clear plan for getting your first position in this growing field.

Common Entry-Level Roles: Finding Your Place

Let's look at the main types of jobs you will find. Even though the titles can be different, most first jobs in data fall into a few groups:

  • Data Analyst: This is the most common starting point. As a data analyst, your main job is to answer questions using data. You will collect data, clean it to make it usable, and then analyze it to find patterns. You will likely use tools like SQL to get data from databases, and then use something like Python, R, or Excel to study it. You will also create charts and dashboards to share what you have found with others in the company.
  • Business Intelligence (BI) Analyst: This role is very similar to a data analyst, but with a special focus on tracking business performance. A BI analyst builds and manages dashboards that show how the company is doing. For example, they might track daily sales, website traffic, or customer satisfaction. This helps company leaders make quick, informed decisions based on the most current information.
  • Junior Data Scientist: This title is sometimes used for roles that are more focused on prediction. A junior data scientist might help build simple machine learning models. For example, they could work on a model that predicts which customers are likely to stop using a service. These jobs often require a stronger understanding of statistics and programming than a typical data analyst role.

Building Your Resume: The Skills Companies Want

Your classes give you knowledge, but companies hire you for your skills. To get a job, you need to show that you can actually do the work. Here are the core skills that hiring managers look for:

  • SQL: This is the language used to communicate with databases. It is the most important technical skill for almost any data job. You absolutely must know SQL. You will be tested on it in interviews and you will use it every day at work.
  • A Programming Language: You need to be comfortable with at least one programming language for data analysis. The most common choices are Python and R. You should know how to use libraries like Pandas in Python or the Tidyverse in R to clean, change, and analyze data.
  • Data Visualization: It is not enough to find something interesting in the data. You have to be able to show it to others in a way that is easy to understand. You should learn how to use a tool like Tableau or Power BI, or use plotting libraries in Python or R to create clear and effective charts.
  • Communication: This is a very important skill. You must be able to explain what you found in the data to people who are not data experts. You need to be able to tell a clear story that helps people understand the information and what it means for the business.

The Power of Projects: Show, Don't Just Tell

A degree shows that you can learn, but a portfolio of your own projects shows that you can do the work. This is the best way to make yourself stand out from other applicants. You do not need many projects. One or two good projects that you can talk about in detail is better than a long list of simple ones. Find a dataset online about a topic you find interesting. Ask a specific question about it. Then, go through the whole process of cleaning the data, analyzing it, and visualizing your results. You can write a short report or blog post about what you did. This gives you something real to show employers and proves you have the skills they need.

Key Takeaways

  • Master the Fundamentals: Focus on getting very good at SQL, a programming language like Python or R, and a visualization tool like Tableau.
  • Build a Portfolio: Complete at least one personal data project from start to finish to prove your skills to employers.
  • Practice Communicating: Work on explaining technical ideas in simple terms. This skill is just as important as your technical abilities.
  • Start with Analyst Roles: Data Analyst and BI Analyst roles are the most common and accessible entry points into the field of data science.

References

  • McKinney, W. (2017). *Python for Data Analysis*. O'Reilly Media.
  • Hardy, T. (2020). *The Senior Data Scientist*. Taylor & Francis.

What Is Economics (Really)?

By Feda Mohammadi | August 25, 2025

Introduction: More Than Just Money

When most people hear the word "economics," they usually think of money, taxes, or the stock market. While those things are part of economics, they are only a small piece of a much bigger puzzle. At its core, economics is the study of how people make choices. It is a social science that looks at how individuals, businesses, and governments decide how to use their limited resources to satisfy their unlimited wants. It is a way of thinking that can help us understand the world in a new and powerful way, from our personal decisions to the biggest global issues.

The Fundamental Problem of Scarcity

The single most important idea in all of economics is scarcity. Scarcity means that we do not have enough resources to produce all the things we want. This is true for everyone. You have a limited amount of time in a day and a limited amount of money in your bank account. A country has a limited number of workers, a limited amount of land, and a limited supply of machines. Because of scarcity, we are forced to make choices. Every time you choose to do something, you are also choosing not to do something else. This is called an opportunity cost. The opportunity cost of any decision is the value of the next best thing you gave up. For example, if you decide to spend two hours watching a movie, the opportunity cost is whatever else you could have done with that time, like studying for a test or working at your job. Economics is the study of how we deal with these trade-offs that scarcity creates.

Microeconomics vs. Macroeconomics: Two Sides of the Same Coin

The field of economics is typically divided into two main branches. They look at the same problems but from different points of view.

  • Microeconomics: This is the study of individual economic actors. Microeconomics looks at the choices made by single people, families, or companies. It asks questions like: How does a company decide what price to charge for its product? How does a family decide how to spend its budget? How do wages get determined for different jobs? It focuses on how supply and demand work together in individual markets to set prices.
  • Macroeconomics: This is the study of the economy as a whole. Macroeconomics looks at the big picture. It asks questions about things that affect the entire country or even the world. For example: What causes inflation, which is the general increase in prices? What causes unemployment? Why do some countries' economies grow much faster than others? Macroeconomics studies things like Gross Domestic Product (GDP), which is the total value of everything a country produces.

Even though they seem different, microeconomics and macroeconomics are closely connected. The health of the whole economy depends on the millions of individual choices made by people and businesses every day.

Thinking Like an Economist: A Way of Seeing the World

More than just a subject, economics is a way of thinking. Economists are trained to look at the world by focusing on a few key ideas. They assume that people are generally rational and respond to incentives. An incentive is anything that motivates someone to act, like a reward or a punishment. For example, if the price of gasoline goes up, people have an incentive to drive less or buy a more fuel efficient car. Economists also spend a lot of time thinking about unintended consequences. Sometimes, a policy that is meant to help can have surprising and negative side effects because of how people react to the new incentives it creates. By understanding these core principles, you can start to see the hidden economic forces that shape our lives and the world around us.

Key Takeaways

  • Economics is the study of choice: It examines how people use limited resources to satisfy unlimited wants.
  • Scarcity is the core problem: Because we can't have everything, we must make trade-offs, which creates opportunity costs.
  • Micro and Macro are two perspectives: Microeconomics focuses on individual choices, while macroeconomics looks at the economy as a whole.
  • It's a way of thinking: Economics is built on ideas like incentives and unintended consequences to explain human behavior.

References

  • Mankiw, N. G. (2020). *Principles of Economics*. Cengage Learning.
  • Heilbroner, R. L. (1999). *The Worldly Philosophers: The Lives, Times, and Ideas of the Great Economic Thinkers*. Simon & Schuster.

What Do Economists Do?

By Feda Mohammadi | August 22, 2025

Introduction: A Career in Understanding Choices

When you tell someone you are studying economics, they might imagine you working at a big bank or on Wall Street. While many economists do work in finance, the field is much broader than that. An economist is like a detective who studies human behavior to understand how the world works. They use theories and data to analyze everything from why a new smartphone costs so much to what policies could help reduce poverty. Their work is found in government, private companies, and universities, and their main goal is always the same. They want to explain how people, businesses, and governments make decisions.

The Three Main Sectors Where Economists Work

Most careers in economics can be found in one of three main areas. Each area has a different focus, but they all rely on the same core skills of analysis and critical thinking.

  • Academia (Universities and Colleges): Many economists with advanced degrees, like a Ph.D., become professors. Their job is split between two main tasks. The first is teaching the next generation of students about the principles of economics. The second, and often larger, part of their job is research. Academic economists try to answer big, new questions about the economy. They might study the effects of immigration on wages, how technology is changing the job market, or the best ways to design environmental policies. They publish their findings in academic journals to share their knowledge with other experts.
  • Government: Economists are essential to the government at every level. They work in many different agencies. For example, at the Federal Reserve, economists analyze data on employment and inflation to help decide on interest rate policies that affect the entire country. At the Department of Justice, they might analyze a proposed merger between two large companies to see if it would be bad for consumers. Others work for agencies like the World Bank or the International Monetary Fund, where they study global economic issues and advise developing countries on how to improve their economies.
  • Private Sector (Businesses): This is the largest and most diverse area of employment for economists. In the private sector, economists use their skills to help companies make better business decisions. A tech company like Amazon or Netflix might hire an economist to help set prices for its products or to design the auctions for its advertising platform. A large bank might hire economists to forecast where the economy is heading so they can make smarter investment choices. They also work in consulting, where they are hired by different companies to solve specific business problems using economic analysis and data.

A Day in the Life: What the Work Looks Like

The daily work of an economist can vary a lot depending on their job, but there are some common activities. A large part of the day often involves working with data. This could mean collecting data from government sources, writing code to clean and analyze it, and building statistical models to test a hypothesis. Another big part of the job is communication. Economists need to be able to explain their complex findings in a simple and clear way. This could involve writing reports, creating presentations with charts and graphs, or giving talks to company leaders or policymakers. The goal is to take complicated information and turn it into a clear story that can be used to make a decision.

The Economist's Toolkit

To do their work, economists rely on a specific set of tools. They have a strong foundation in economic theory, including both microeconomics and macroeconomics. They also have strong quantitative skills. This includes a deep understanding of statistics and econometrics, which is the use of statistical methods to analyze economic data. In today's world, technical skills are also very important. Most economists are proficient in at least one programming language, like R or Python, which they use for their data analysis. This combination of theoretical knowledge, statistical skill, and technical ability allows them to tackle a wide range of interesting and important problems.

Key Takeaways

  • Economists study decision making: They analyze how people, firms, and governments make choices with limited resources.
  • They work everywhere: The main employment sectors are academia (research and teaching), government (policy analysis), and the private sector (business strategy).
  • The job is data driven: A typical day involves collecting, analyzing, and interpreting data to answer questions.
  • Communication is key: A crucial part of the job is explaining complex findings in a simple way to help others make better decisions.

References

  • Bureau of Labor Statistics, U.S. Department of Labor. (2023). *Occupational Outlook Handbook, Economists*.

Your First Data Science Toolkit: Python vs. R

By Feda Mohammadi | August 19, 2025

Introduction: The Great Debate

When you start learning about data science, you will quickly hear about one of the biggest debates in the field. Which programming language should you learn first? The two most popular languages for data work are Python and R. Both languages are free, powerful, and have large communities of users who create helpful tools. However, they were created for different reasons and have different strengths. Deciding which one to learn first can feel like a big choice. This guide will explain the differences between Python and R to help you decide which one is right for you.

R: The Statistician's Native Language

The R language was built by statisticians specifically for doing statistical analysis and making graphs. If you have a background in statistics or social science, the way R works might feel very natural to you. It is designed to follow the workflow of an analyst.

  • Strengths: R's biggest advantage is its amazing collection of packages for almost any statistical method you can think of. If a new statistical method is invented, it is often available as an R package first. A famous collection of packages called the Tidyverse makes cleaning and working with data very organized and powerful. For making charts and graphs, the R package ggplot2 is considered the best tool available by many experts.
  • Weaknesses: R can sometimes feel a little strange if you try to use it for general programming tasks that are not related to data. Its style is different from many other common programming languages, which can make it a little harder to learn other languages later on.
  • Who is it for? R is a perfect choice for students who plan to go into academic research or want to become a dedicated statistician.

Python: The Versatile All-Purpose Powerhouse

Python was created as a general purpose language, with a focus on being easy to read and simple to use. It became popular for data science more recently, after a set of powerful libraries were developed that made it great for data analysis and especially for machine learning.

  • Strengths: Python's biggest advantage is that it can be used for almost anything. It is like a Swiss Army knife for programming. You can use it for data analysis, but also for building websites, automating tasks, and much more. This makes it a very valuable skill. The language is known for being easy for beginners to learn. The main libraries for data science, like Pandas for working with data tables and Scikit-learn for machine learning, are very strong and widely used in the industry.
  • Weaknesses: Although Python's tools for making charts are good, many people feel that R's ggplot2 is more elegant and intuitive for creating complex graphs.
  • Who is it for? Python is an excellent choice for students who want a flexible language they can use for many different things, or for those who are very interested in machine learning and artificial intelligence.

The Verdict: Which Should You Choose?

The honest answer is that you cannot make a bad choice. Both Python and R are excellent languages for data work. Many professional data scientists know how to use both and choose the best tool for each specific project. Learning one language makes it much easier to learn the other one later.

However, if you are a student starting today and need one recommendation, **you should probably start with Python.**

For most people entering the job market now, Python is the more strategic choice. Because it is so versatile, it opens up more job opportunities. Its dominance in the growing field of machine learning makes it a very valuable skill for the future. After you become comfortable doing data analysis in Python, you will find it very easy to learn the basics of R if you ever need to for a specific class or job.

Key Takeaways

  • R is for specialized statistical analysis: It is the best tool for academic research and complex statistics, with the best-in-class tools for data visualization.
  • Python is the versatile industry standard: It is a general-purpose language that is excellent for data science, machine learning, and many other tasks, making it a very flexible skill.
  • Start with Python: For most students, Python is the recommended first language because it is easier to learn and opens up more career opportunities.
  • You can't go wrong: The most important thing is to learn one language well. The skills you learn will easily transfer to the other.

References

  • VanderPlas, J. (2016). *Python Data Science Handbook*. O'Reilly Media.
  • Wickham, H., & Grolemund, G. (2017). *R for Data Science*. O'Reilly Media.

Top Skills You Need To Become A Data Scientist

By Feda Mohammadi | August 16, 2025

Introduction: More Than Just Coding

Becoming a data scientist is a goal for many students who enjoy math, statistics, and programming. It is a rewarding career where you get to solve interesting puzzles that can have a real impact on a business or the world. But being a successful data scientist requires more than just knowing how to write code. It involves a unique mix of technical knowledge, analytical thinking, and strong communication abilities. The best data scientists are not just good with computers. They are also great thinkers and storytellers. This guide will break down the most important skills you need to develop to build a successful career in data science.

The Foundation: Technical Skills

These are the practical, hands on abilities that you need to work with data every day. Without a solid technical foundation, it is impossible to do the job.

  • Programming (Python and R): As discussed in another article, you need to be proficient in a programming language built for data. Python is the most common choice in the industry because it is versatile and has powerful libraries like Pandas for data manipulation and Scikit-learn for machine learning. R is also very popular, especially in academia and research, for its deep statistical capabilities. You should aim to be very comfortable in at least one of these.
  • Databases (SQL): Data is almost always stored in databases. SQL is the universal language used to get information out of these databases. A data scientist who cannot write SQL is like a chef who cannot use a knife. It is an absolutely essential skill for collecting the raw material you need for your analysis.
  • Mathematics and Statistics: You do not need to be a math genius, but you must have a strong understanding of core concepts from statistics and linear algebra. You need to understand what a p-value means, how a regression model works, and the basics of probability. This knowledge is what allows you to choose the right analytical method for a problem and to understand the results of your models.

The Core of the Job: Analytical Skills

These skills are about how you think. They are what allow you to take a messy, real world problem and turn it into a structured data project.

  • Critical Thinking: This is the ability to look at a problem from many different angles and to question your own assumptions. A good data scientist does not just accept the data as it is. They ask critical questions like: Where did this data come from? Could there be any errors or biases in it? Is this the right data to answer the question I have?
  • Problem Solving: Often, you will be given a vague business goal, like "we want to increase customer engagement." A data scientist's job is to translate that vague goal into a specific, answerable question that can be addressed with data. This requires creativity and a structured way of thinking to break a big problem down into smaller, manageable parts.

The Differentiator: Soft Skills

These are the skills that separate a good data scientist from a great one. They are all about how you work with other people.

  • Communication: This might be the single most important skill of all. You can build the most brilliant machine learning model in the world, but if you cannot explain how it works and what its results mean to a non-technical audience, it is useless. You must be able to tell a clear and convincing story with your data.
  • Curiosity: Data scientists are naturally curious people. They enjoy digging into data to understand why things are the way they are. They are always asking "why?" and looking for the next interesting question to investigate. This curiosity is what drives them to find insights that other people might miss.
  • Business Acumen: This means understanding how a business works and what is important to its success. A great data scientist does not just analyze data for fun. They focus on the problems that are most important to the company. Understanding the company's goals helps you to focus your efforts on work that will have the biggest impact.

Key Takeaways

  • It's a Mix of Skills: A great data scientist combines technical, analytical, and soft skills.
  • Master the Technical Trio: Focus on becoming strong in Programming (Python or R), Databases (SQL), and Statistics.
  • Think Critically: The best analysis comes from questioning assumptions and deeply understanding the business problem.
  • Communication is Your Superpower: Your value is determined not by what you find, but by how well you can explain it to others to drive action.

References

  • Davenport, T. H., & Patil, D. J. (2012). *Data Scientist: The Sexiest Job of the 21st Century*. Harvard Business Review.
  • O'Neil, C., & Schutt, R. (2013). *Doing Data Science: Straight Talk from the Frontline*. O'Reilly Media.
