Landing Your First Job in Econ & Data Science

By Feda Mohammadi | September 1, 2025

Stepping into the job market with a background in economics and data science is an exciting prospect. You possess a unique and powerful blend of analytical rigor from economics and technical skill from data science, a combination that is in extremely high demand across countless industries. But navigating the entry-level landscape can feel like trying to find a needle in a haystack. The sheer number of job titles can be overwhelming: Data Analyst, Research Analyst, Financial Analyst, Business Intelligence Analyst... the list goes on. How do you know which path is right for you? Let’s break down the landscape, demystify the roles, and give you a concrete roadmap for landing that first great job.

Common Entry-Level Roles: Finding Your Fit

First, let’s understand the core functions behind the titles. While a specific role can vary by company and industry, most entry-level positions fall into a few key categories:

  • Data Analyst: This is the most common entry point and a fantastic way to build foundational skills. As a data analyst, you are a detective. You'll be responsible for collecting, cleaning, exploring, and analyzing data to uncover insights that answer business questions. A typical day might involve writing SQL queries to pull data, using Python or R to perform statistical analysis, creating dashboards in tools like Tableau or Power BI to track key metrics, and presenting your findings to help leaders make informed decisions. Your economics background gives you a massive edge here because you don't just see numbers; you understand the underlying economic principles of supply and demand, incentives, and market behavior that drive those numbers.
  • Research Assistant/Analyst: This role is prevalent in academia, think tanks (like the Brookings Institution), and government agencies (like the Federal Reserve or the World Bank). It involves supporting larger research projects under the guidance of senior economists or researchers. You might be tasked with running complex statistical models (econometrics is key here!), conducting thorough literature reviews to understand the existing body of knowledge, and helping to prepare charts, tables, and sections for academic papers or policy reports. It's a perfect fit if you enjoy deep, focused analysis and are considering graduate school.
  • Financial Analyst: If you're drawn to the world of finance, this role uses your skills to analyze financial statements, build forecasting models to predict company earnings or market trends, and assess the value of stocks, bonds, and other investment opportunities. You'll apply economic principles directly to understand market dynamics, interest rate movements, and company performance within the broader economy.
  • Business Analyst: This role often serves as a crucial bridge between the technical data team and other business departments like marketing, sales, or operations. You'll use data to understand business processes, identify pain points or opportunities for improvement, and recommend concrete solutions. This position requires strong communication and stakeholder management skills in addition to your technical abilities, as you'll be translating business needs into data questions and data insights back into business strategy.

The Skills That Actually Get You Hired

A degree gets your resume looked at, but demonstrable skills get you the job. Regardless of the title, hiring managers are looking for a core set of competencies. Your goal is to showcase these skills through your coursework, projects, and internship experiences.

  • Technical Proficiency: You must be comfortable with the tools of the trade. SQL is non-negotiable. You will be asked about it in interviews, and you will use it on the job. Beyond that, you need a strong command of a scripting language. The choice is typically between Python (with its powerful libraries like Pandas, NumPy, Matplotlib, and Scikit-learn) or R (especially the Tidyverse ecosystem). Being proficient in at least one is essential.
  • Statistical and Econometric Knowledge: This is where your economics major shines. You must have a solid grasp of core concepts like regression analysis (linear and logistic), hypothesis testing, confidence intervals, and probability. Be prepared to explain a concept like p-value or R-squared in simple terms. This demonstrates that you understand the "why" behind your models, not just how to run the code.
  • Communication and Visualization: Finding a brilliant insight is useless if you can't explain it clearly to someone who isn't a data expert. You must be able to tell a compelling story with data. Practice creating clean, uncluttered, and insightful charts in tools like Tableau, or with libraries like Python's Matplotlib/Seaborn or R's ggplot2. Being able to walk someone through your analysis in a simple, narrative-driven way is perhaps the most underrated skill in the field.

Your Secret Weapon: The Portfolio Project

A degree proves you can learn, but a portfolio of projects proves you can *do*. This is your chance to stand out. A club like BEDSA is the perfect place to start. Collaborate on a project, or find a topic you're passionate about and do your own analysis. Find a compelling public dataset from sources like Kaggle, the World Bank, FRED (Federal Reserve Economic Data), or a government open data portal. Ask a clear, interesting question. Then, walk through the entire process: data cleaning, exploratory analysis, modeling, and summarizing your findings. Document your work in a GitHub repository with clean code and a detailed README file, or write a blog post about it. A single, well-executed project that you can talk about in depth during an interview is often more impressive than a long list of coursework. Your portfolio is your proof, so start building it today.

Correlation vs. Causation: A Trap for Young Players

By Feda Mohammadi | August 28, 2025

In the world of data, there is one concept so fundamental, yet so frequently misunderstood, that it forms a great dividing line between novice and expert. That concept is the crucial difference between correlation and causation. As students of economics and data science, mastering this distinction isn't just an academic exercise—it is a critical thinking skill that separates sloppy, dangerous analysis from sound, actionable insight. Getting this wrong can lead to absurd conclusions, wasted resources, and terrible policy decisions. It's the essential trap that every aspiring analyst must learn to spot and avoid.

What is Correlation? It's Just a Relationship.

At its core, correlation is simple. It's a statistical measure that describes a relationship or connection between two variables. When one variable changes, the other tends to change in a predictable way. We can have:

  • Positive Correlation: When one variable increases, the other also tends to increase. A classic example is the relationship between temperature and ice cream sales. As the thermometer rises in the summer, the cash registers at ice cream shops ring more frequently.
  • Negative Correlation: When one variable increases, the other tends to decrease. Think about the relationship between the number of hours a student studies and the number of errors they make on an exam. Generally, more study time is associated with fewer mistakes.

Finding correlation is often the first, exciting step in data analysis. It's relatively easy to calculate and can point us toward interesting patterns that are worthy of further investigation. The great danger lies in stopping there. It's tempting to see a strong correlation and immediately jump to a conclusion about what caused it. This is where the trouble begins.

What is Causation? It's a Much Bolder Claim.

Causation, or causality, is a far more powerful and difficult-to-prove claim. It means that a change in one variable directly *causes* a change in another. There is an underlying mechanism connecting the two. For a correlation to be causal, we must be certain that the effect wouldn't have occurred without the cause and that there isn't some other factor at play. For example, flipping a light switch *causes* the light to turn on. Pushing on the gas pedal *causes* a car to accelerate. There's a direct, verifiable link.

The Lurking Variable: Ice Cream, Crime, and Summer Heat

Let’s explore the most famous example used to illustrate this fallacy. If you analyze city-level data, you will almost certainly find a strong positive correlation between ice cream sales and the crime rate. On days when ice cream sales are high, crime rates are also high. A naive analysis would conclude that eating ice cream causes people to commit crimes. The policy recommendation would be absurd: should we ban ice cream to make our cities safer?

Of course not. This is a textbook case of a "lurking" or "confounding" variable. In this situation, the lurking variable is the weather, specifically the temperature. When it's hot outside, two things happen independently: 1) more people buy ice cream to cool down, and 2) more people are out of their homes, walking around, and interacting in public spaces, which increases the opportunity for social friction and, sadly, crime. The heat causes an increase in *both* variables, creating a spurious correlation between them, but one does not cause the other. The ice cream isn't the problem; the heat is the common cause.

Why This is the Central Challenge of Economics

This concept is the absolute bedrock of modern econometrics and applied economics. Economists are constantly trying to determine if a policy, event, or action caused a specific outcome, or if the relationship is merely correlational. We can't run controlled experiments for most of the big questions we care about. We can't create two identical countries, apply a policy to one, and see what happens. So, we have to use data and clever statistical methods to try and untangle these complex relationships. Consider these classic economic questions:

  • Did a recent increase in the minimum wage *cause* unemployment to rise, or were both the wage hike and the job losses driven by a broader economic downturn that was already underway?
  • Does attending college *cause* people to have higher lifetime earnings? Or is it that the type of people who are admitted to and complete college (perhaps more ambitious, intelligent, or from more privileged backgrounds) would have earned more anyway, and college is just a signal of those pre-existing traits?

To get closer to answering these questions, economists and data scientists use a sophisticated toolkit. This includes techniques like randomized controlled trials (RCTs), where feasible, which are the gold standard for establishing causality. It also includes methods like regression analysis (where we can "control for" the effects of other variables, like the weather in our ice cream example), instrumental variables, and regression discontinuity designs. The entire goal is to isolate the true causal impact of one variable on another. So next time you see a headline that says "Study shows X is linked to Y," put on your analyst hat and ask the crucial question that separates the amateurs from the pros: Is it just a correlation, or is it causation?

Your First Data Science Toolkit: Python vs. R

By Feda Mohammadi | August 25, 2025

As you dive into the exciting world of data science, you'll quickly encounter one of the field's foundational debates: which programming language should you learn first? The two undisputed champions, the titans of the data world, are Python and R. Both are free, open-source, powerful, and have massive, supportive communities that have created an incredible ecosystem of tools and packages. But they were born from different philosophies and have distinct strengths. Choosing which one to start with can feel like a daunting decision. So, which one should you invest your time in? Let's break down the great Python vs. R debate.

R: The Statistician's Native Language

R was built by statisticians, for statisticians. Its primary purpose, from its inception, has been statistical analysis and data visualization. If you come from a background heavy in econometrics or social science research, R's syntax and data structures (like data frames and vectors) might feel very natural and intuitive. It's designed around the workflow of an analyst.

  • Core Strengths: R's greatest strength is its unparalleled ecosystem of packages for nearly every statistical method imaginable. If a new, cutting-edge statistical technique is published in an academic paper, chances are it will be available as an R package first. The Tidyverse, a collection of packages designed for data science that share a common design philosophy, is a work of art. Packages like ggplot2 for data visualization are widely considered the gold standard for creating publication-quality graphics with a concise and logical grammar. Packages like dplyr and tidyr make the often-painful process of data cleaning and manipulation elegant and powerful. For many complex statistical tasks, academic research, and bioinformatics, R remains the undisputed king.
  • Potential Weaknesses: R can be a bit quirky when used as a general-purpose programming language. Its syntax is different from most other popular languages (like Java, C++, or Python), which can make it a bit harder to pick up other languages later. While it's possible to build web apps (with Shiny) or put models into production with R, it's generally less common and the tooling is less mature than in the Python ecosystem.
  • Who is it for? Students who are certain they want to go into academic research, a Ph.D. program, or a role as a dedicated statistician or quantitative analyst will feel perfectly at home with R.

Python: The Versatile All-Purpose Powerhouse

Python was designed from the start as a general-purpose programming language with a focus on code readability and simplicity. Its popularity in data science is a more recent phenomenon, exploding with the development of a suite of powerful libraries that made it a fantastic environment for data analysis and, especially, machine learning.

  • Core Strengths: Python's number one advantage is its incredible versatility. It's the Swiss Army knife of programming. You can use it for data analysis, but you can also use it for web development (with frameworks like Django and Flask), scripting and automation, game development, and more. This makes it an incredibly valuable skill to have on your resume. Its syntax is clean, consistent, and generally considered easier for complete beginners to learn. The core data science libraries, often called the "PyData" stack, are robust and mature: Pandas (for data manipulation, inspired by R's data frames), NumPy (for numerical operations), Matplotlib and Seaborn (for plotting), and Scikit-learn (the go-to library for machine learning). When it comes to building and deploying machine learning models at scale, Python is the industry standard.
  • Potential Weaknesses: While Python's visualization libraries are very powerful, many analysts argue that R's ggplot2 allows for the creation of complex and beautiful plots with more intuitive code. For some very niche statistical methods, a package might be available in R before it is in Python.
  • Who is it for? Students who want a versatile "do-it-all" language, are particularly interested in machine learning and AI, or see themselves working in a tech company where they might need to collaborate with software engineers, will find Python to be an excellent choice.

The Verdict: You Can't Lose, But Here's a Recommendation

Here's the honest, and perhaps unsatisfying, answer: **you can't go wrong with either.** Both are excellent choices, they are more similar than they are different, and many data professionals know how to use both, picking the right tool for the job. Learning one makes learning the other much easier.

However, if you're a student starting today and need a recommendation, here it is: Start with Python.

For most students entering the job market now, Python is the more strategic first choice. Its versatility opens up a wider range of job opportunities, and its clear dominance in the rapidly growing fields of machine learning and AI makes it an invaluable, future-proof skill. Since it's also a fantastic general-purpose language, the programming fundamentals you learn are more broadly applicable across the tech landscape. Once you are comfortable with data analysis in Python, you'll be able to pick up R's Tidyverse in a weekend if a specific project or course requires it.

Remember, the ultimate goal is not to become a great Python or R programmer, but to become a great analyst, a great problem-solver, and a great thinker. The language is just the tool. Pick one, master the fundamentals of cleaning, exploring, modeling, and communicating with data, and you'll be well on your way to a successful career.

Beyond Adam Smith: Minds Shaping Modern Economics

By Feda Mohammadi | August 22, 2025

When you begin your journey into economics, you inevitably start with one towering figure: Adam Smith. As the 18th-century Scottish philosopher and author of "The Wealth of Nations," he is rightfully hailed as the father of modern economics, the champion of free markets, and the originator of the powerful "invisible hand" metaphor. While his contributions were monumental and laid the very foundation of the field, economics has evolved dramatically over the past 250 years. It is a living, breathing discipline that constantly questions its own assumptions. To truly understand the economic debates of today, you need to know the intellectual giants who challenged old ideas, responded to new crises, and forged entirely new paths of inquiry.

John Maynard Keynes: The Architect of Macroeconomics

If Adam Smith represents the classical view of serene, self-regulating markets, John Maynard Keynes represents the revolution born from chaos. Writing during the worldwide economic catastrophe of the Great Depression in the 1930s, Keynes looked at the world and saw that classical theories were failing. He argued forcefully that economies could get stuck in long, painful periods of high unemployment, and that waiting for the "invisible hand" to fix things was not a viable option. He contended that, contrary to classical belief, prices and wages were "sticky" downwards and wouldn't adjust quickly enough to restore the economy to full employment. His transformative idea, laid out in his masterwork, "The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money," was that in a severe downturn, the government must step in to manage the economy. He argued that governments should use fiscal policy—increasing government spending or cutting taxes—to boost aggregate demand and jolt the economy back to life. In doing so, Keynes didn't just offer a solution; he created the entire field of macroeconomics, which studies the economy as a whole.

Milton Friedman: The Counter-Revolutionary

For decades after Keynes, his ideas dominated economic policy. But in the latter half of the 20th century, a powerful counter-argument emerged, led by Milton Friedman. A Nobel laureate and leader of the "Chicago school" of economics, Friedman argued that Keynesian government intervention often did more harm than good. He was a staunch advocate for free markets, individual liberty, and a limited role for government. Friedman's most influential contribution was in monetary policy. He argued that inflation was "always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon," caused by central banks printing too much money. He advocated for a steady, predictable growth rate in the money supply, rather than the activist, fine-tuning approach of the Keynesians. His ideas became incredibly influential in the 1970s and 1980s, shaping the policies of leaders like Ronald Reagan and Margaret Thatcher and providing the intellectual foundation for the modern focus on central bank independence and inflation targeting.

Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky: The Psychologists Who Remade Economics

For centuries, the fundamental building block of economic theory was the assumption of *homo economicus*—a perfectly rational, self-interested person who always makes decisions to flawlessly maximize their own well-being. But is that how real people behave? Psychologists Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky conducted a series of brilliant experiments that showed this assumption was fundamentally, and predictably, wrong. They demonstrated that real humans are subject to a host of systematic cognitive biases and often rely on mental shortcuts (heuristics) that lead to irrational choices. For example, they showed we are profoundly loss-averse (we feel the pain of a $100 loss much more than the pleasure of a $100 gain) and that we are easily swayed by how choices are "framed." Their work gave birth to the incredibly influential field of behavioral economics, which combines insights from psychology and economics to create far more realistic models of human decision-making. For this work, Kahneman won the Nobel Prize in Economics in 2002 (Tversky had sadly passed away several years earlier).

Esther Duflo: The Pioneer of a New, Data-Driven Development Economics

For decades, the debate over how to help the world's poorest populations was dominated by grand theories with surprisingly little empirical evidence. On one side were those who argued for massive aid infusions, and on the other were those who argued aid created dependency. Esther Duflo and her colleagues Abhijit Banerjee and Michael Kremer brought a new, scientific rigor to this debate. They championed the use of Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs)—the same method used in medicine to test the effectiveness of a new drug—to figure out which specific anti-poverty interventions actually work on the ground. Instead of asking a vague question like "Does foreign aid work?", they asked specific, testable questions like, "What is the most cost-effective way to convince parents in rural Kenya to deworm their children?" or "Do free, insecticide-treated bed nets actually reduce malaria rates and improve school attendance?" This evidence-based, micro-level approach has revolutionized development economics, moving it from abstract ideological debates to a practical, data-driven science of finding what works. For this transformative work, they were jointly awarded the Nobel Prize in Economics in 2019.
